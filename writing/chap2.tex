\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW}
This chapter reviews existing studies related to machine learning-based transformer fault classification using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA). The purpose of this chapter is to establish the theoretical and technical foundation for the proposed study by examining how recent research has addressed fault classification, feature representation, dataset limitations, and model interpretability in DGA-based transformer diagnostics.

The review is organized around four main themes. First, it examines the emergence of machine learning approaches for transformer fault classification and the shift from conventional pattern interpretation toward data-driven modelling \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024, Nguyen2025}. Second, it discusses the machine learning workflow used in DGA studies, with emphasis on feature representation, preprocessing, and dataset characteristics \parencite{Peng2024, Lin2026, Du2024}. Third, it evaluates persistent challenges such as class imbalance, limited labeled data, and generalization across datasets and operating conditions \parencite{Chen2023, Zhou2023, Wang2023, Laburu2024}. Finally, it reviews explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) and physically interpretable approaches that aim to make diagnostic outputs more meaningful for engineering use \parencite{Nguyen2025, Souvannalath2024, Vidal2023}.

Rather than listing studies independently, this chapter compares how different methods address the same diagnostic problem and highlights the main trade-offs reported in the literature. These comparisons are used to identify the research gap that motivates the proposed study.

\section{Overview of Transformer Fault Diagnosis Using DGA}
Machine learning has become a major approach for transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA), where gas concentration patterns are used to classify transformer fault types. Recent studies show that machine learning models are effective because the relationships among dissolved gases and fault categories are nonlinear and difficult to capture using simple threshold-based reasoning. Ren et al. demonstrated that an optimized CatBoost model can improve multi-class fault classification performance \parencite{Ren2025}, while Kumar et al. showed through comparative evaluation that multiple machine learning algorithms are suitable for DGA-based fault identification \parencite{Kumar2024}. Nguyen et al. further showed that adaptive fuzzy logic can produce interpretable classification decisions aligned with transformer operating conditions \parencite{Nguyen2025}. However, these studies also reveal a limitation in the current literature: strong reported results are often presented as evidence of effectiveness without equivalent attention to robustness across different data conditions or to the practical interpretability of the final model. As a result, the shift toward machine learning has improved predictive capability, but it has not fully resolved the reliability and usability requirements of transformer diagnostics.

However, the literature also shows that higher predictive capability does not automatically translate into a robust and practical diagnostic system. The main limitations reported across studies are not only model accuracy, but also data quality, imbalanced fault classes, limited labeled samples, and the difficulty of interpreting model outputs in a physically meaningful way. Du et al., Kari et al., and Chen et al. showed that class imbalance directly affects the reliability of multiclass classification results \parencite{Du2024, Kari2023, Chen2023}. Zhou et al., Sun et al., and Liu et al. showed that limited labeled data forces researchers to adopt semi-supervised or structure-aware learning strategies \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025, Liu2025}. Wang et al. and Souvannalath et al. further highlighted that practical deployment requires diagnostic outputs that are understandable and trustworthy in engineering settings \parencite{Wang2023, Souvannalath2024}. Taken together, these studies suggest that many existing models are technically promising but still operationally incomplete. In other words, much of the literature addresses individual weaknesses in isolation, while the broader diagnostic problem remains only partially solved.

\section{Emergence of Machine Learning in DGA-Based Fault Classification}
The reviewed literature shows that supervised multi-class classification remains the dominant formulation for DGA-based transformer fault diagnosis. Studies such as Ren et al. and Kumar et al. focused on learning a direct mapping from gas concentration features to discrete fault categories and reported improved predictive performance when machine learning models are properly optimized and evaluated \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024}. Nguyen et al. approached the same task from an interpretable perspective through adaptive fuzzy logic, showing that transparent decision rules can still be competitive when designed around domain knowledge \parencite{Nguyen2025}. This suggests that the field has developed along two parallel directions: performance-oriented classification models and interpretable rule-oriented models. The limitation is that these two directions are often pursued separately, which weakens the practical value of both. Performance-oriented studies frequently provide limited explanation, whereas interpretable approaches may not demonstrate equally strong performance under more demanding classification settings.

The literature also shows that this supervised formulation is no longer sufficient when DGA datasets are small, imbalanced, or only partially labeled. Zhou et al. used graph convolutional networks to exploit relationships among labeled and unlabeled samples \parencite{Zhou2023}, while Sun et al. proposed a dual-stage semi-supervised framework to improve classification under limited-label conditions \parencite{Sun2025}. Liu et al. similarly explored self-organizing neural structures to model data patterns without depending entirely on labeled observations \parencite{Liu2025}. Compared with standard supervised approaches, these methods shift the focus from pure fault prediction to representation learning under data constraints. Even so, the literature still does not show clearly whether these methods generalize reliably across datasets with different characteristics, which limits the strength of the performance claims being made.

This transition is important because it shows that progress in DGA-based diagnosis is increasingly tied to how well a model deals with data limitations rather than to classifier choice alone. In other words, the literature has evolved from asking which classifier performs best to asking how diagnostic performance can be maintained when the dataset is incomplete, imbalanced, or weakly labeled.

\section{Machine Learning Workflow for DGA Analysis}
Although different studies use different algorithms, most DGA-based machine learning systems follow a similar workflow consisting of data acquisition, preprocessing, feature representation, model training, and evaluation. Within this workflow, the literature shows that preprocessing and feature construction often have a decisive effect on diagnostic performance. Peng et al. proposed a multi-dimensional gas representation to capture interactions among gases for early warning \parencite{Peng2024}, while Lin et al. used manifold-based feature selection to reduce redundancy and improve generalization \parencite{Lin2026}. Ren et al. further showed that optimized feature inputs improve the performance and stability of a CatBoost classifier \parencite{Ren2025}. These studies suggest that performance gains are often achieved not only through model architecture, but also through better representation of DGA information.

The workflow perspective also reveals a methodological issue in the literature: many studies report strong classification results, but the relative contribution of preprocessing, feature engineering, sampling strategy, and model design is not always clearly separated. This makes it difficult to determine whether improvement comes from the classifier itself or from upstream data handling choices. As a result, comparing methods across studies becomes challenging, especially when datasets, preprocessing pipelines, and evaluation settings differ. This is a significant weakness in the literature because it reduces the transparency of methodological comparisons and makes reported performance gains harder to interpret critically.

Another recurring issue is model evaluation. Laburu et al. showed that conventional metrics such as accuracy may be insufficient for imbalanced DGA datasets \parencite{Laburu2024}. Zhou et al. and Wang et al. further indicated that changes in data distribution can alter model behaviour across datasets and operating conditions \parencite{Zhou2024, Wang2023}. Therefore, a complete machine learning workflow for DGA analysis must be judged not only by reported accuracy but also by its treatment of data imbalance, feature relevance, and robustness of evaluation.

\section{Learning Paradigms in Transformer Diagnostics}
The literature can be grouped into several learning paradigms, each addressing a different limitation of DGA-based fault diagnosis. The first and most established paradigm is supervised classification, where the main objective is to assign each DGA sample to a known fault category. This paradigm is represented by models such as CatBoost and other comparative machine learning classifiers that aim to maximize discrimination among fault classes \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024}. Its main strength is strong predictive performance when labeled data are available, but its weakness is heavy dependence on dataset quality and class balance. In practice, this means that its reported effectiveness can be overstated when evaluation conditions are controlled more tightly than real deployment conditions.

The second paradigm is semi-supervised and structure-aware learning, which has emerged to address limited labeled data. Zhou et al. and Sun et al. showed that incorporating unlabeled samples into the learning process can improve performance when fully annotated DGA datasets are not available \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025}. These methods are valuable in practical settings because real transformer monitoring data are often easier to collect than to label. However, their effectiveness still depends on whether the learned structure reflects meaningful fault patterns rather than dataset-specific noise. This creates an important uncertainty: better use of unlabeled data does not necessarily mean better diagnostic validity.

The third paradigm is hybrid and knowledge-guided learning, where representation learning or contextual reasoning is combined with classification. Vidal and Castro used variational autoencoders and neural networks to improve feature learning \parencite{Vidal2023}, while Souvannalath et al. integrated contextual knowledge into a cognitive artificial intelligence framework \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. Compared with purely supervised models, these approaches attempt to improve both adaptability and interpretability. The literature therefore suggests that recent progress lies less in replacing one algorithm with another and more in combining learning paradigms to address weaknesses in data, performance, and explanation.

\section{Feature Representation and Characteristics in DGA Analysis}
Feature representation is a central issue in DGA-based transformer diagnostics because the quality of the input representation determines how well a model can distinguish among fault mechanisms. Gas concentrations are physically meaningful variables, but their diagnostic value depends on how relationships among gases are encoded. Wang et al. showed that interactions among gas components are nonlinear \parencite{Wang2023}, while Nguyen et al. demonstrated that interpretable rule-based systems can still exploit these relationships when the feature logic is carefully designed \parencite{Nguyen2025}. This indicates that DGA features are not merely numerical inputs; they represent fault behaviour and therefore must be selected or transformed in a way that preserves diagnostic meaning.

The reviewed studies show two main directions in feature representation. One direction relies on engineered features and explicit feature selection. Peng et al. proposed multi-dimensional gas representation to capture interactions among gases \parencite{Peng2024}, and Lin et al. applied manifold-based feature selection to retain informative attributes while reducing redundancy \parencite{Lin2026}. These methods improve model efficiency and often strengthen generalization by reducing irrelevant variation.

The second direction emphasizes data-driven representation learning. Vidal and Castro used variational autoencoders to learn compact DGA representations \parencite{Vidal2023}, while Zhou et al. and Sun et al. relied on semi-supervised strategies to extract structure from both labeled and unlabeled data \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025}. Compared with manual feature construction, learned representations may better capture hidden relationships in complex DGA data. However, they can also reduce interpretability if the learned features are not clearly linked back to known gas generation mechanisms. This trade-off between representational power and interpretability remains a recurring issue throughout the literature, and it is not adequately resolved by simply showing that a learned representation improves predictive accuracy.

\section{Datasets Used in DGA-Based Transformer Fault Classification}
The literature consistently shows that dataset characteristics strongly influence diagnostic outcomes. Studies such as Ren et al. and Kumar et al. used DGA datasets with multiple fault categories to train and compare classifiers \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024}, while Wang et al. highlighted that changes in dataset composition and distribution can affect model behaviour \parencite{Wang2023}. These findings imply that reported model performance must be interpreted in relation to the dataset used, rather than treated as a universal indicator of diagnostic capability.

Among dataset-related issues, class imbalance is the most frequently reported. In many DGA datasets, some fault types are rare, causing models to favour majority classes. Du et al. addressed this problem with GAN-based data augmentation \parencite{Du2024}, Kari et al. improved this idea through ACGAN-based balancing \parencite{Kari2023}, and Chen et al. proposed SE-ELM for highly imbalanced multiclass diagnosis \parencite{Chen2023}. These studies confirm that imbalance is not a minor preprocessing issue but a structural limitation that shapes how performance should be interpreted.

At the same time, the literature suggests that imbalance handling methods introduce their own trade-offs. Augmentation-based solutions can improve minority-class representation, but they add modelling complexity and depend on the quality of generated samples. Algorithm-level solutions can improve classification stability, but may still remain sensitive to the underlying data distribution. Therefore, the dataset problem in DGA-based diagnosis is not solved simply by adding more samples; it requires methods that improve minority-class reliability without undermining model trustworthiness. This is where many existing studies remain limited, because they report better balance-related performance without fully examining whether the improvement is stable, realistic, or transferable.

\section{Challenges in ML-Based DGA Fault Classification}
The main challenges reported in the literature are closely connected rather than independent. Class imbalance reduces reliability across fault categories \parencite{Du2024, Kari2023, Chen2023}, limited labeled data constrains supervised learning \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025, Liu2025}, and differences in dataset distribution weaken generalization across operating conditions \parencite{Zhou2024, Wang2023}. Together, these issues explain why strong performance in one experimental setting does not necessarily guarantee robust diagnostic performance in another. A key weakness of the existing literature is that these challenges are often discussed separately, even though in practice they interact and compound one another.

Another important challenge is evaluation. Many studies report improved accuracy, but accuracy alone may hide poor minority-class performance or unstable behaviour under data shift. Laburu et al. explicitly questioned the adequacy of conventional evaluation metrics for imbalanced datasets \parencite{Laburu2024}. This is important because DGA-based diagnosis is a safety-relevant task, and a model that performs well on average but fails on underrepresented fault categories may still be unsuitable in practice. Consequently, evaluation practices in the current literature are often not strict enough to justify strong claims about diagnostic reliability.

Overall, the literature indicates that improving transformer fault classification requires more than selecting a stronger algorithm. It requires an approach that jointly considers data constraints, evaluation strategy, and model behaviour across fault categories. This broader view is necessary to move from promising experimental performance to a more reliable diagnostic framework.

\section{Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and Physical Interpretability}
Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) has become increasingly important in DGA-based transformer diagnosis because engineering adoption depends on whether model outputs can be justified. Wang et al. noted that hybrid AI models may lack transparency in their decision-making process \parencite{Wang2023}, which limits their practical usefulness even when predictive performance is high. In contrast, Nguyen et al. showed that adaptive fuzzy logic can produce interpretable diagnostic rules based on gas features \parencite{Nguyen2025}, and Souvannalath et al. demonstrated that contextual and cognitive reasoning can improve the diagnostic meaning of model outputs \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. These studies show that interpretability is not an optional add-on, but a functional requirement in transformer diagnostics.

The literature also makes an important distinction between generic interpretability and physical interpretability. In DGA applications, explanations should be consistent with known transformer fault mechanisms, such as the association of acetylene with arcing faults and methane or ethylene with thermal faults. A model may be explainable in a technical sense yet still be weak from an engineering perspective if its reasoning does not align with known gas generation behaviour. For this reason, physically meaningful interpretation is more demanding than post-hoc explanation alone.

Recent work attempts to bridge this gap. Vidal and Castro combined representation learning with model interpretability \parencite{Vidal2023}, while Souvannalath et al. explicitly integrated contextual knowledge into the diagnostic process \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. However, the literature still suggests that high-performing and physically interpretable systems are often developed separately. This separation helps explain why the integration of performance and interpretability remains a central unresolved issue. In effect, many studies acknowledge the need for explainability, but fewer demonstrate that explainability is achieved without weakening classification effectiveness or practical applicability.

\section{Transition Toward Intelligent and Explainable Systems}
Recent research shows a clear movement toward diagnostic systems that are not only accurate but also adaptive and explainable. Semi-supervised models improve learning under limited labeled data \parencite{Sun2025, Zhou2023}, hybrid representation-learning approaches improve feature extraction \parencite{Vidal2023}, and cognitive AI frameworks bring domain knowledge into the reasoning process \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. These developments indicate that the field is moving beyond isolated classifier optimization toward more complete diagnostic frameworks.

Even so, the literature remains fragmented. Some studies emphasize classification performance, others emphasize imbalance handling, and others focus on interpretability or domain consistency. Few studies address these requirements within one coherent framework. As a result, the transition toward intelligent and explainable systems is visible, but not yet fully consolidated into a model that is simultaneously accurate, robust to dataset limitations, and physically meaningful for practical transformer diagnosis. This fragmentation is a major reason why the current body of work still falls short of providing a sufficiently complete solution for real-world transformer fault classification.

\section{Research Gap}
Based on the reviewed literature, a clear gap remains in the development of machine learning-based transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA).

First, although previous studies have reported promising results, there is still a need to improve the overall classification performance of DGA-based diagnostic models. Existing approaches such as CatBoost, semi-supervised learning, and hybrid feature learning have shown that performance can be improved, but these gains are often demonstrated under specific dataset conditions. The literature also shows that model effectiveness is influenced by feature representation, data quality, and dataset variation, indicating that more reliable and robust fault classification performance is still required for practical application \parencite{Ren2025, Sun2025, Vidal2023, Wang2023, Zhou2024}. Thus, the current literature provides evidence of potential improvement, but not yet enough evidence of consistently reliable performance under realistic diagnostic conditions.

Second, class imbalance remains an unresolved issue in DGA datasets. Although data augmentation and imbalance-aware models have been proposed, many approaches rely on additional model complexity or synthetic data generation, which may affect reliability and efficiency. As a result, there is still a need for a classification framework that can handle imbalanced fault classes effectively while maintaining stable performance across different categories \parencite{Du2024, Kari2023, Chen2023}. Current studies have improved the treatment of imbalance, but they have not fully eliminated the risk of biased or unstable prediction across minority fault classes.

Third, the literature shows limited emphasis on ensuring that model outputs remain interpretable and consistent with known transformer fault mechanisms. In practical transformer diagnostics, prediction results should not only be accurate but also align with physical gas generation behavior associated with specific fault types. However, many current models focus mainly on predictive performance, with less attention given to physically meaningful interpretation and practical diagnostic trustworthiness \parencite{Nguyen2025, Souvannalath2024, Wang2023}. This means that even when a model performs well numerically, its diagnostic value may remain limited if engineers cannot judge whether the result is physically credible.

Therefore, there is a need for a machine learning-based DGA fault classification approach that improves classification performance, addresses class imbalance effectively, and provides interpretable outputs that are consistent with transformer fault mechanisms.

\section{Research Objectives}
Based on the identified research gap, this study proposes the following research objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item To develop a machine learning-based model for classifying transformer faults using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA) data with improved and more reliable classification performance.
    \item To design an approach that addresses class imbalance in DGA datasets in order to improve classification stability across different fault categories.
    \item To incorporate interpretable analysis into the proposed model so that the classification results are consistent with known transformer fault mechanisms.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Chapter Summary}
This chapter reviewed recent studies on machine learning-based transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA). The review showed that machine learning has improved DGA-based fault classification by learning complex relationships among gas features, but the literature also indicates that diagnostic performance depends heavily on feature representation, dataset quality, and evaluation strategy.

The chapter further showed that current research has progressed through several complementary directions, including supervised classification, semi-supervised learning, imbalance-aware modelling, feature learning, and interpretable or knowledge-guided approaches. However, these directions are often developed to solve separate problems rather than combined into a unified diagnostic framework.

Overall, the literature indicates three unresolved issues: the need for more reliable classification performance, better handling of class imbalance, and stronger integration of interpretability with known transformer fault mechanisms. These issues define the research gap and provide the foundation for the proposed methodology in the next chapter.
