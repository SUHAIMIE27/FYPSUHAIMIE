\chapter{Review of Machine Learning Approaches for DGA-Based Transformer Fault Diagnosis}
This chapter reviews recent studies on machine learning-based transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA). The purpose of the review is to establish the conceptual and technical basis for the proposed study by examining how prior work has addressed fault classification, feature representation, dataset constraints, model evaluation, and interpretability in DGA-based diagnostics. The review focuses specifically on recent studies that formulate transformer fault diagnosis as a multiclass machine learning problem and that evaluate model usefulness in relation to both predictive performance and engineering interpretability.

The review is organised around four connected themes. First, it examines the shift from conventional ratio-based and rule-based interpretation toward machine learning-based fault classification \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024, Nguyen2025}. Second, it analyses the machine learning workflow used in DGA studies, with emphasis on preprocessing, feature representation, and model evaluation \parencite{Peng2024, Lin2026, Du2024, Laburu2024}. Third, it discusses persistent dataset-related challenges, especially class imbalance, limited labelled data, and variation across datasets and operating conditions \parencite{Chen2023, Zhou2023, Wang2023, Zhou2024}. Finally, it reviews interpretability-oriented work that attempts to make diagnostic outputs more meaningful for engineering use \parencite{Nguyen2025, Souvannalath2024, Vidal2023}.

Rather than presenting studies as isolated contributions, this chapter compares them in terms of what diagnostic problem they solve, what assumptions they make about the data, and what limitations remain after their reported improvements. This comparative approach is used to identify the research gap that motivates the proposed study.

\section{Overview of Transformer Fault Diagnosis Using DGA}
Dissolved Gas Analysis is widely used in transformer condition monitoring because different fault processes produce different gas generation patterns in insulating oil. In practice, DGA is used to distinguish among conditions such as partial discharge, low-energy discharge, high-energy discharge, and thermal faults at different temperature ranges. This makes DGA a useful basis for fault diagnosis, but it also means that interpretation depends on recognising complex relationships among multiple gas components rather than on any single gas concentration alone. In recent years, machine learning has become a major approach for interpreting these patterns, particularly when the relationship between gas concentrations and fault categories is nonlinear and difficult to capture using fixed thresholds or simple ratio methods. Ren et al. showed that an optimised CatBoost model can improve multiclass fault classification performance \parencite{Ren2025}, while Kumar et al. reported that several machine learning classifiers can be used effectively for DGA-based fault identification when suitable input features and evaluation procedures are applied \parencite{Kumar2024}. Nguyen et al. approached the same diagnostic task from an adaptive fuzzy logic perspective and demonstrated that interpretability can remain central to the modelling process \parencite{Nguyen2025}.

These studies collectively confirm that machine learning is now a credible alternative to conventional DGA interpretation. However, they also show that predictive improvement alone does not automatically produce a robust diagnostic system. Across the literature, strong reported performance is often accompanied by unresolved concerns related to dataset quality, minority fault representation, limited labels, and the practical meaning of model outputs. Du et al., Kari et al., and Chen et al. linked class imbalance directly to unstable multiclass performance \parencite{Du2024, Kari2023, Chen2023}, while Zhou et al., Sun et al., and Liu et al. showed that limited labelled data changes the learning problem itself and pushes researchers toward semi-supervised or structure-aware methods \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025, Liu2025}. Wang et al. and Souvannalath et al. further highlighted the need for diagnostic outputs that are not only accurate but also understandable in engineering contexts \parencite{Wang2023, Souvannalath2024}. The broader implication is that the field has improved classification capability, but many models remain only partially aligned with practical diagnostic requirements.

\section{Emergence of Machine Learning in DGA-Based Fault Classification}
The reviewed literature shows that supervised multiclass classification remains the dominant formulation for DGA-based transformer fault diagnosis. In this formulation, the objective is to learn a direct mapping from DGA measurements to discrete fault categories. Ren et al. and Kumar et al. represent the performance-oriented branch of this literature, where the main concern is improving discrimination among known fault classes through classifier choice, optimisation, and evaluation \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024}. In contrast, Nguyen et al. represent a more interpretable branch in which decision transparency is treated as a design requirement rather than a secondary consideration \parencite{Nguyen2025}.

This distinction is important because it reveals a recurring divide in the literature. Studies that prioritise predictive performance often improve class separation and average classification accuracy, but they usually provide limited explanation of why a model reaches its diagnostic conclusion. By contrast, studies that emphasise interpretability preserve clearer decision logic, yet may not demonstrate the same level of predictive strength under more difficult multiclass settings. The result is not simply a difference in modelling style, but a difference in what each study defines as success. From a practical transformer diagnostic perspective, both aims matter, and the separation between them weakens the completeness of existing solutions.

The literature also shows that the standard supervised formulation becomes less adequate when DGA datasets are small, imbalanced, or only partially labelled. Zhou et al. used graph convolutional networks to exploit structure across labelled and unlabelled samples \parencite{Zhou2023}, while Sun et al. proposed a dual-stage semi-supervised strategy to improve classification when labels are limited \parencite{Sun2025}. Liu et al. likewise explored self-organising neural structures to model data regularities beyond strictly supervised learning \parencite{Liu2025}. Compared with conventional classifier comparison studies, these works shift attention away from selecting the best classifier in isolation and toward learning useful structure under realistic data constraints. Even so, the literature is still less clear on whether these methods generalise reliably across datasets with different compositions and operating conditions. As a result, the improvement they report is promising, but not yet fully established as robust diagnostic improvement.

\section{Machine Learning Workflow for DGA Analysis}
Although the reviewed studies use different algorithms, most follow a broadly similar workflow involving data acquisition, preprocessing, feature construction or selection, model training, and performance evaluation. Within this workflow, the literature suggests that the quality of upstream data handling strongly influences downstream classification results. Peng et al. proposed a multi-dimensional gas representation to better capture interactions among dissolved gases \parencite{Peng2024}, while Lin et al. used manifold-based feature selection to reduce redundancy and retain diagnostically useful attributes \parencite{Lin2026}. Ren et al. further showed that careful preparation of feature inputs can improve both the performance and stability of a CatBoost classifier \parencite{Ren2025}. Together, these studies indicate that performance gains often depend as much on representation design as on the classifier itself.

This workflow perspective also exposes a methodological weakness in the literature. In DGA studies, preprocessing may include scaling, normalisation, outlier handling, resampling for class balance, or transformation of raw gas values into ratios or composite features. Evaluation may likewise involve different choices of train-test split, cross-validation procedure, and metrics such as accuracy, recall, F1-score, or confusion-matrix-based analysis. Many studies report strong classification results without clearly separating the effect of preprocessing, feature engineering, data balancing, and model design. When these elements are changed simultaneously, it becomes difficult to judge which component is responsible for the reported gain. That lack of separation weakens comparison across studies, because two models may appear to differ by algorithm while actually differing just as much in sampling strategy, feature transformation, or evaluation setup.

Model evaluation is another recurring issue. Laburu et al. argued that conventional metrics such as overall accuracy may be inadequate for imbalanced DGA datasets \parencite{Laburu2024}. Zhou et al. and Wang et al. further suggested that changes in data distribution can alter model behaviour across datasets and operating environments \parencite{Zhou2024, Wang2023}. Therefore, a complete machine learning workflow for DGA analysis should not be judged only by its highest reported accuracy, but also by how transparently it handles feature relevance, class imbalance, and robustness of evaluation.

\section{Learning Paradigms in Transformer Diagnostics}
The literature can be grouped into several learning paradigms, each reflecting a different response to the limitations of DGA-based diagnosis. The first and most established paradigm is supervised classification. Its main strength is direct predictive performance when labelled data are sufficient and the class structure is well represented, as shown in studies based on CatBoost and other comparative classifiers \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024}. Its weakness is that performance claims are highly sensitive to dataset quality, class balance, and evaluation design.

The second paradigm is semi-supervised and structure-aware learning. Zhou et al. and Sun et al. demonstrated that unlabelled samples can be incorporated into the learning process to improve classification when fully annotated datasets are not available \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025}. This is valuable because transformer monitoring data are often easier to collect than to label. However, these methods depend on the assumption that the structure learned from available data reflects real fault relationships rather than dataset-specific artefacts. Their benefit is therefore conditional: better use of unlabelled data does not necessarily mean better diagnostic validity under new conditions.

The third paradigm is hybrid and knowledge-guided learning. Vidal and Castro used representation learning through variational autoencoders together with neural classification \parencite{Vidal2023}, while Souvannalath et al. incorporated contextual reasoning into a cognitive artificial intelligence framework \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. Compared with purely supervised models, these approaches attempt to address more than one problem at once, typically by improving adaptability, feature learning, or interpretability. Their strength is broader problem coverage, but their limitation is higher methodological complexity and more difficult validation. The literature therefore suggests that recent progress is less about replacing one classifier with another and more about combining paradigms to respond to weaknesses in data, explanation, and practical usability.

\section{Feature Representation and Characteristics in DGA Analysis}
Feature representation is central to DGA-based fault classification because the diagnostic value of the input does not depend only on the raw gas concentrations, but also on how relationships among gases are encoded for learning. Wang et al. indicated that the interaction among gas components is nonlinear \parencite{Wang2023}, while Nguyen et al. showed that these relationships can also be expressed through interpretable rule-based logic when the feature design is aligned with domain knowledge \parencite{Nguyen2025}. This means that DGA features should not be treated as generic numerical variables alone; they are diagnostically meaningful measurements whose representation influences both predictive performance and interpretability.

The reviewed studies suggest two main directions in feature handling. The first is engineered representation with explicit feature selection. In this direction, studies work with raw gas concentrations, gas ratios, or manually constructed composite features designed to emphasise relations among gases. Peng et al. proposed a multi-dimensional gas representation to capture interaction patterns \parencite{Peng2024}, and Lin et al. applied manifold-based feature selection to retain informative features while reducing redundancy \parencite{Lin2026}. The strength of this direction is that it can improve efficiency and generalisation while preserving a clearer link between input variables and diagnostic reasoning.

The second direction is data-driven representation learning. Vidal and Castro used variational autoencoders to learn compact DGA representations \parencite{Vidal2023}, while Zhou et al. and Sun et al. extracted structure from both labelled and unlabelled data through semi-supervised learning \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025}. These methods may capture hidden relationships that are difficult to encode manually, especially when the data structure is complex. However, their main limitation is interpretability: once features are learned in a latent form, it becomes harder to show whether the representation remains consistent with recognised transformer fault behaviour. This trade-off between representational power and engineering interpretability remains insufficiently resolved in the literature.

\section{Datasets Used in DGA-Based Transformer Fault Classification}
Dataset characteristics strongly influence the reported performance of DGA-based diagnostic models. Ren et al. and Kumar et al. used multiclass DGA datasets to compare or optimise classifiers \parencite{Ren2025, Kumar2024}, while Wang et al. emphasised that changes in dataset composition and distribution can alter model behaviour \parencite{Wang2023}. These findings indicate that reported classification results should be interpreted relative to the data used, rather than treated as universally transferable evidence of diagnostic superiority.

Another point emerging from the literature is that DGA datasets are not uniform in size, class definition, or source. Some studies rely on relatively small or institution-specific datasets, while others use benchmark datasets that are repeatedly reused for model comparison. Publicly available datasets help reproducibility, but repeated use of the same benchmark data can make performance comparisons appear stronger than they would under external validation. Private or utility-specific datasets may better reflect operational conditions, yet they often reduce comparability across studies.

Among dataset-related issues, class imbalance is the most persistent. In many DGA datasets, some fault categories occur much less frequently than others, causing models to favour majority classes and inflating average performance. Du et al. addressed this problem using GAN-based augmentation \parencite{Du2024}, Kari et al. further explored ACGAN-based balancing \parencite{Kari2023}, and Chen et al. proposed SE-ELM for highly imbalanced multiclass diagnosis \parencite{Chen2023}. These studies show that imbalance is not a minor preprocessing inconvenience, but a structural issue that directly shapes how model performance should be interpreted.

At the same time, the literature shows that imbalance handling is not a complete solution in itself. Augmentation-based approaches may improve minority-class representation, but their success depends on whether the generated samples are realistic enough to preserve diagnostic validity. Algorithm-level approaches may improve stability, but they can remain sensitive to the original data distribution. Therefore, the dataset problem in DGA-based diagnosis is not solved simply by increasing sample count or by balancing class frequencies numerically. The stronger requirement is to improve minority-class reliability without reducing model trustworthiness or overfitting to dataset-specific patterns.

\section{Challenges in ML-Based DGA Fault Classification}
The main challenges identified in the literature are interconnected rather than independent. Class imbalance reduces reliability across fault categories \parencite{Du2024, Kari2023, Chen2023}, limited labelled data constrains fully supervised learning \parencite{Zhou2023, Sun2025, Liu2025}, and distributional variation weakens generalisation across datasets and operating conditions \parencite{Zhou2024, Wang2023}. These challenges compound one another. For example, a model trained on a small and imbalanced dataset is more vulnerable not only to poor minority-class performance, but also to instability when applied to data from another source. A major weakness of the current literature is that these issues are often discussed separately even though practical deployment requires them to be handled jointly.

Evaluation is a further challenge. Many studies report improved accuracy, but accuracy alone can conceal weak minority-class performance and unstable behaviour under data shift. Laburu et al. explicitly questioned the adequacy of conventional metrics for imbalanced DGA datasets \parencite{Laburu2024}. This matters because transformer fault diagnosis is a safety-relevant application: a model that performs well on average but fails on rare yet important fault categories may still be unacceptable in practice. A related weakness is the limited use of external validation or cross-dataset testing, which makes it difficult to judge whether reported performance extends beyond the original experimental setting. Consequently, some current claims of strong diagnostic performance are not fully convincing unless supported by more discriminating evaluation.

Overall, the literature suggests that improving transformer fault classification requires more than selecting a stronger algorithm. It requires a framework that addresses data limitations, evaluation design, and fault-wise model behaviour in an integrated manner. This broader perspective is necessary if DGA-based diagnosis is to move from promising experimental results toward more dependable real-world use.

\section{Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and Physical Interpretability}
Explainable Artificial Intelligence has become increasingly important in DGA-based transformer diagnosis because engineering adoption depends on whether a model's output can be justified. Wang et al. noted that hybrid AI models may remain opaque in their decision process \parencite{Wang2023}, which limits practical trust even when predictive performance is strong. In contrast, Nguyen et al. showed that adaptive fuzzy logic can express more transparent diagnostic rules based on gas features \parencite{Nguyen2025}, while Souvannalath et al. demonstrated that contextual and cognitive reasoning can improve the diagnostic meaning of model outputs \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. These studies indicate that interpretability is not merely an optional enhancement, but a functional requirement in engineering diagnosis.

The literature also suggests that generic interpretability is not enough. In transformer diagnostics, explanations should be meaningful in relation to known fault behaviour rather than only mathematically descriptive. For example, if a model identifies gases associated with discharge or thermal degradation as influential for a given classification, that explanation is more useful than a purely abstract indication of feature importance without engineering context. A model may therefore be explainable in a technical sense and still be weak from an engineering perspective if its reasoning cannot be related back to recognised transformer fault mechanisms. This makes physical interpretability more demanding than post-hoc explanation alone and highlights the difference between built-in interpretability, such as fuzzy rules, and post-hoc explanation applied after classification.

Recent work attempts to narrow this gap. Vidal and Castro combined representation learning with interpretability-oriented modelling \parencite{Vidal2023}, while Souvannalath et al. explicitly integrated contextual knowledge into the reasoning process \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. Even so, the literature still tends to separate high performance from strong physical interpretability. This explains why the integration of predictive effectiveness and engineering meaning remains one of the central unresolved issues in the field.
Recent studies nevertheless show a broader transition toward diagnostic systems that are not only accurate, but also adaptive and explainable. Semi-supervised models seek to improve learning under limited labelled data \parencite{Sun2025, Zhou2023}, hybrid representation-learning approaches seek to extract more useful fault information from DGA inputs \parencite{Vidal2023}, and cognitive AI frameworks seek to embed contextual reasoning into diagnosis \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. However, these directions remain fragmented: some studies focus primarily on classification performance, others on imbalance handling, and others on interpretability or domain consistency. Only a limited number of studies attempt to address these requirements together within one coherent framework. As a result, the movement toward intelligent and explainable DGA diagnosis is visible, but it has not yet produced a sufficiently integrated solution that is simultaneously accurate, robust to dataset limitations, and meaningful for engineering decision-making. This unresolved integration problem directly motivates the proposed study.

\section{Research Gap}
Based on the reviewed literature, a clear gap remains in machine learning-based transformer fault diagnosis using DGA.

First, although many studies report improved performance, the literature does not yet provide strong evidence that these gains remain reliable across different dataset conditions. Existing approaches such as CatBoost, semi-supervised learning, and hybrid representation learning have demonstrated potential, but their reported effectiveness is often tied to particular datasets, feature designs, or evaluation settings \parencite{Ren2025, Sun2025, Vidal2023, Wang2023, Zhou2024}. Therefore, a gap remains in developing a classification approach that offers not only high performance, but also stronger reliability under realistic diagnostic conditions.

Second, class imbalance remains an unresolved structural problem in DGA datasets. Although augmentation-based and imbalance-aware methods have been proposed, many of them depend on synthetic sample generation or additional model complexity, and the stability of their improvement is not always clear \parencite{Du2024, Kari2023, Chen2023}. This indicates a continuing need for a framework that can improve minority-class reliability without undermining overall trustworthiness or efficiency.

Third, the literature still gives limited emphasis to ensuring that model outputs remain interpretable and consistent with known transformer fault behaviour. In practical transformer diagnostics, prediction results should not only be numerically accurate, but also meaningful enough for engineers to assess their credibility. However, much of the current literature continues to prioritise predictive performance over physically meaningful interpretation and diagnostic trustworthiness \parencite{Nguyen2025, Souvannalath2024, Wang2023}. This creates a gap between model success in experiments and model usefulness in practice.

Taken together, the literature shows that existing studies usually strengthen one of these requirements at a time: some improve performance, some address imbalance, and others improve interpretability. Fewer studies show how these three requirements can be addressed together within one coherent classification framework. Therefore, there is a need for a machine learning-based DGA fault classification approach that improves classification performance, addresses class imbalance effectively, and provides interpretable outputs that remain meaningful for transformer fault diagnosis.

\section{Research Objectives}
Based on the identified research gap, this study proposes the following research objectives:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item To develop a machine learning-based model for classifying transformer faults using DGA data with improved and more reliable classification performance.
    \item To design an approach that addresses class imbalance in DGA datasets in order to improve classification stability across different fault categories.
    \item To incorporate interpretable analysis into the proposed model so that the classification results remain meaningful in relation to transformer fault behaviour and can be assessed more confidently in practical diagnosis.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Chapter Summary}
This chapter reviewed recent studies on machine learning-based transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis. The review showed that machine learning has improved DGA-based fault classification by learning complex relationships among gas features, but it also showed that reported performance depends heavily on feature representation, dataset quality, class balance, and evaluation strategy.

The chapter further showed that current research has progressed through several complementary directions, including supervised classification, semi-supervised learning, imbalance-aware modelling, feature learning, and interpretable or knowledge-guided systems. However, these directions are often developed to address separate limitations rather than combined into a single diagnostic framework.

Overall, the literature indicates three unresolved issues: the need for more reliable classification performance, better handling of class imbalance, and stronger integration of interpretability with practical transformer diagnosis. These issues define the research gap and provide the foundation for the proposed methodology in Chapter 3, where the model development, imbalance-handling strategy, and interpretability design will be presented.
