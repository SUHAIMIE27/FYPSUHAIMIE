\chapter{LITERATURE REVIEW}

This chapter critically reviews existing studies related to machine learning-based transformer fault classification using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA). The purpose of this chapter is to provide a comprehensive understanding of current methodologies, techniques, and challenges associated with DGA-based transformer diagnostics. By synthesizing findings from previous research, this chapter establishes a strong theoretical and technical foundation for the proposed study.

The first section examines the application of machine learning techniques in transformer fault diagnosis, including classification models, semi-supervised learning, and data-driven approaches. These methods are widely utilized to model the relationship between gas concentration patterns and corresponding fault types \parencite{Abhang_20161, alma2016, Nguyen2025}. The second section focuses on key components of ML-based diagnostic systems, including feature representation, dataset characteristics, and data preprocessing techniques such as data augmentation and feature selection \parencite{Du2024, Lin2026, Peng2024}. Additionally, critical challenges such as class imbalance, limited labeled data, and model generalization are discussed based on findings from previous studies \parencite{Chen2023, Zhou2023, Wang2023}.

Furthermore, this chapter explores the role of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) in enhancing the interpretability of machine learning models for transformer diagnostics. Existing studies demonstrate that the integration of domain knowledge with data-driven models can significantly improve both diagnostic reliability and practical applicability \parencite{Nguyen2025, Souvannalath2024}.

Finally, this chapter identifies key research gaps in current approaches, particularly in the integration of high-accuracy classification models with interpretable and physically meaningful diagnostic frameworks. These limitations highlight the need for a more unified approach, which forms the basis for the proposed methodology presented in the subsequent chapter.

\section{Overview of Transformer Fault Diagnosis Using DGA}

Machine learning (ML) has been extensively applied in transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA), where gas concentration data are utilized to classify fault types. Ren et al. proposed a CatBoost-based fault classification model optimized using SCGWO, demonstrating enhanced classification performance across multiple transformer fault categories \parencite{Ren2025}. Similarly, Kumar et al. conducted a comparative study of various machine learning models for DGA-based transformer fault classification, confirming the effectiveness of classification approaches in identifying fault types \parencite{Kumar2024}. Nguyen et al. further applied adaptive fuzzy logic for DGA-based diagnosis, providing interpretable rule-based classification aligned with transformer fault conditions \parencite{Nguyen2025}. These studies collectively demonstrate that ML-based classification has become a fundamental approach in transformer fault diagnosis.

However, a major limitation identified across these studies is the presence of imbalanced datasets, which can significantly affect model performance. Du et al. addressed this issue by proposing a reduced-dimensional generative adversarial network (GAN) to generate synthetic minority-class samples, thereby improving class distribution in DGA datasets \parencite{Du2024}. Kari et al. further developed an improved auxiliary classifier GAN (ACGAN) model to enhance data balance and diagnostic performance under imbalanced conditions \parencite{Kari2023}. In addition, Chen et al. proposed a sequential ensembled extreme learning machine (SE-ELM) specifically designed for highly imbalanced multiclass DGA classification, demonstrating improved classification stability across both majority and minority fault classes \parencite{Chen2023}. Despite these advancements, most approaches introduce additional model complexity, which may affect computational efficiency and practical deployment.

Another limitation in DGA-based ML models is the availability of labeled data. Zhou et al. proposed a semi-supervised graph convolutional network (GCN) that utilizes both labeled and unlabeled DGA data, improving classification performance under limited labeled conditions \parencite{Zhou2023}. Similarly, Sun et al. introduced a dual-stage semi-supervised learning framework to enhance diagnostic performance when labeled fault data are insufficient \parencite{Sun2025}. Liu et al. also applied self-organizing neural networks to model DGA data structures without relying entirely on labeled samples \parencite{Liu2025}. These studies indicate that transformer fault diagnosis is not only a classification problem but also a data-constrained learning problem, where data availability directly impacts model performance.

Feature representation has also been identified as a critical factor affecting model performance. Peng et al. proposed a multi-dimensional gas representation approach to improve early fault warning capability by capturing relationships among multiple gas features \parencite{Peng2024}. Lin et al. applied manifold-based feature selection to identify relevant gas features and reduce redundancy in transformer fault diagnosis \parencite{Lin2026}. Ren et al. further demonstrated that optimized feature inputs improve the performance of CatBoost-based models for DGA classification \parencite{Ren2025}. While these approaches enhance classification accuracy, many rely on dataset-specific feature engineering, which may limit generalization across different transformer conditions.

More advanced approaches have explored data-driven feature learning techniques. Vidal and Castro proposed a hybrid model combining variational autoencoders (VAE) and neural networks to learn compact feature representations from DGA data \parencite{Vidal2023}. Zhou et al. applied graph-based learning to capture relationships between samples in semi-supervised settings \parencite{Zhou2023}. Sun et al. further demonstrated that semi-supervised frameworks can extract useful representations from both labeled and unlabeled datasets \parencite{Sun2025}. These methods indicate a shift from predefined gas features toward learned representations, although such models may reduce interpretability.

Despite improvements in classification accuracy, interpretability remains a critical challenge. Wang et al. highlighted that hybrid AI models for DGA-based diagnosis may lack transparency in their decision-making process \parencite{Wang2023}. Nguyen et al. demonstrated that fuzzy logic models provide interpretable diagnostic rules based on gas features \parencite{Nguyen2025}. Souvannalath et al. proposed a cognitive artificial intelligence framework that integrates domain knowledge with data-driven learning to improve diagnostic reasoning \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. These findings suggest that interpretability should be aligned with known gas generation mechanisms and transformer fault behavior to ensure practical applicability.

Overall, the reviewed studies demonstrate that ML has significantly enhanced transformer fault classification using DGA. However, the literature consistently highlights key challenges, including class imbalance, limited labeled data, feature representation, and model interpretability. Addressing these challenges is essential for developing reliable, efficient, and practically deployable transformer diagnostic systems.

\section{Emergence of Machine Learning in DGA-Based Fault Classification}

In the reviewed literature, transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA) is commonly formulated as a multi-class classification problem, where gas concentration features are mapped to discrete fault categories. Ren et al. proposed a CatBoost-based classification model optimized using SCGWO for transformer fault diagnosis, explicitly addressing multi-class classification using DGA data \parencite{Ren2025}. Kumar et al. conducted a comparative evaluation of multiple machine learning algorithms for transformer fault classification, demonstrating the applicability of classification models across different fault categories \parencite{Kumar2024}. Nguyen et al. applied adaptive fuzzy logic systems to classify transformer faults based on gas concentrations, providing interpretable rule-based classification aligned with transformer fault conditions \parencite{Nguyen2025}. These studies collectively demonstrate that classification-based approaches are consistently employed in DGA-based fault diagnosis. However, most of these methods rely heavily on dataset-specific characteristics, which may limit their generalization capability in real-world transformer conditions.

In addition to supervised classification, several studies address the limitation of labeled data by introducing semi-supervised learning frameworks. Zhou et al. developed a semi-supervised graph convolutional network (GCN) that utilizes both labeled and unlabeled DGA data for transformer fault diagnosis \parencite{Zhou2023}. Sun et al. proposed a dual-stage semi-supervised learning framework to improve classification performance under limited labeled data conditions \parencite{Sun2025}. Liu et al. applied self-organizing neural networks to model DGA data structures without requiring fully labeled datasets \parencite{Liu2025}. These approaches indicate that DGA-based fault diagnosis is not solely a classification task but also a data-constrained learning problem, where the availability of labeled data significantly influences model performance.

Another consistent issue identified across the literature is class imbalance in DGA datasets. Du et al. addressed this issue by proposing a reduced-dimensional generative adversarial network to generate synthetic minority-class samples, thereby improving dataset distribution \parencite{Du2024}. Kari et al. developed an improved auxiliary classifier GAN (ACGAN) to balance class distribution and enhance classification performance \parencite{Kari2023}. Chen et al. formulated transformer fault diagnosis as a highly imbalanced multiclass problem and proposed a sequential ensembled extreme learning machine (SE-ELM) to improve classification stability across fault categories \parencite{Chen2023}. While these approaches improve performance under imbalanced conditions, they often introduce additional model complexity, which may affect computational efficiency and practical deployment. Therefore, handling class imbalance remains a critical aspect of machine learning problem formulation in DGA-based transformer fault diagnosis.

However, despite the extensive application of machine learning techniques in DGA-based transformer fault diagnosis, several limitations remain evident. Most existing approaches focus primarily on improving classification performance, while challenges such as class imbalance, limited labeled data, and dataset dependency continue to affect model robustness and generalization. In addition, although semi-supervised and data augmentation techniques have been introduced, these methods often increase model complexity without fully addressing practical deployment constraints. Furthermore, the integration of high-performance classification models with interpretable and physically meaningful diagnostic frameworks remains limited. 

Therefore, a significant research gap exists in developing a unified machine learning-based DGA fault classification framework that not only achieves high accuracy but also ensures interpretability, effectively handles imbalanced and limited data, and aligns model predictions with known transformer fault mechanisms. Addressing these limitations forms the basis for the proposed methodology in this study.

Feature selection and dimensionality reduction are also necessary to address the high dimensionality of DGA datasets. Zhou et al. proposed a semi-supervised graph convolutional network (GCN) that utilizes both labeled and unlabeled data to improve learning performance under limited supervision \parencite{Zhou2023}. While these approaches improve model performance, many existing workflows rely on dataset-specific preprocessing and feature engineering strategies, which may limit their robustness and generalization across different transformer operating conditions. Furthermore, the integration of efficient feature engineering techniques with interpretable and scalable machine learning models remains limited. Therefore, effective feature representation and selection are not only essential components of the ML workflow but also key challenges that need to be addressed in developing practical transformer fault diagnosis systems.

In DGA-based transformer fault classification, features represent measurable characteristics of transformer conditions derived from gas generation processes. \section{Challenges in ML-Based DGA Fault Classification}

Despite the reported performance of ML-based approaches, several challenges remain in DGA-based transformer fault diagnosis. One major issue is class imbalance, where certain fault types are underrepresented in the dataset. Du et al. addressed this problem by proposing a generative adversarial network to generate synthetic minority-class samples \parencite{Du2024}. Kari et al. further developed an improved auxiliary classifier GAN (ACGAN) to enhance dataset balance and improve classification performance \parencite{Kari2023}. Chen et al. proposed a sequential ensembled extreme learning machine (SE-ELM) specifically designed for highly imbalanced multiclass DGA datasets, improving classification stability across fault categories \parencite{Chen2023}. 

While these approaches improve performance under imbalanced conditions, they often introduce increased model complexity and may rely on synthetic data that does not fully reflect real transformer operating conditions. Consequently, imbalance-aware modeling remains a critical yet unresolved challenge for achieving reliable and practically deployable fault classification systems.

Another challenge is model evaluation and generalization across different datasets and operating conditions. Laburú et al. demonstrated that conventional evaluation metrics such as accuracy may not be sufficient for imbalanced datasets and proposed alternative evaluation measures to better assess classification performance \parencite{Laburu2024}. Zhou et al. reported that model performance can vary significantly across different datasets due to variations in data distribution \parencite{Zhou2024}. Wang et al. further showed that hybrid AI models are highly sensitive to dataset characteristics, which directly influence diagnostic accuracy \parencite{Wang2023}. 

These findings indicate that robust evaluation strategies and strong generalization capability are essential for practical deployment. However, many existing studies are validated on limited or dataset-specific scenarios, which restricts their applicability in real-world transformer environments. Therefore, addressing both evaluation robustness and generalization remains a key challenge that must be considered in the development of effective ML-based transformer diagnostic systems.

\section{Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and Physical Interpretability}

Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) has been increasingly applied in ML-based transformer fault diagnosis to enhance the interpretability of model predictions. In DGA-based applications, interpretability refers to the ability of a model to provide explanations that are consistent with known gas generation mechanisms associated with transformer faults. Wang et al. demonstrated that hybrid AI models may lack transparency in their decision-making processes, which can limit their practical adoption \parencite{Wang2023}. Nguyen et al. applied adaptive fuzzy logic systems to produce interpretable rule-based outputs based on gas features \parencite{Nguyen2025}. Souvannalath et al. further proposed a cognitive artificial intelligence framework that integrates domain knowledge with data-driven learning to improve diagnostic reasoning \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. These studies collectively demonstrate that interpretability is essential for the reliable application of ML models in transformer diagnostics.

Several studies have proposed approaches that integrate interpretability with model performance. Souvannalath et al. introduced a cognitive AI approach that combines contextual reasoning with machine learning models \parencite{Souvannalath2024}. Vidal and Castro developed a hybrid model combining variational autoencoders and genetic programming to improve feature representation and model interpretability \parencite{Vidal2023}. Sun et al. demonstrated that semi-supervised learning frameworks can enhance both classification performance and the understanding of data structure in DGA datasets \parencite{Sun2025}. These approaches indicate a shift toward embedding interpretability within model design rather than treating it as a separate post-processing step. 

However, many high-performance machine learning models remain inherently complex and function as black-box systems, making it difficult to fully interpret their decision-making processes. This creates a trade-off between model accuracy and interpretability, which remains a significant challenge in practical transformer diagnostic applications.

In DGA-based fault diagnosis, interpretability must also be consistent with physical fault mechanisms. Gas generation patterns are associated with specific fault types, and diagnostic models should reflect these relationships. For example, acetylene is associated with arcing faults, while methane and ethylene are associated with thermal faults. Ensuring that model outputs align with these relationships improves the reliability and trustworthiness of diagnostic decisions. Wang et al. emphasized that model transparency is necessary for engineering applications \parencite{Wang2023}, while Nguyen et al. demonstrated that rule-based systems can effectively reflect domain knowledge in diagnostic outputs \parencite{Nguyen2025}. 

Therefore, XAI in DGA-based transformer diagnosis must not only improve interpretability but also ensure consistency between model predictions and known physical behavior. Achieving this balance remains a key challenge and highlights the need for integrated approaches that combine high model performance with meaningful and physically interpretable outputs.

\section{Research Gap}

Based on the reviewed literature, several critical limitations remain in machine learning-based transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA).

Firstly, although many studies have achieved high classification accuracy using advanced machine learning models such as CatBoost, GAN-based augmentation, and semi-supervised learning, most approaches primarily focus on performance optimization without adequately addressing model interpretability. While techniques such as fuzzy logic and cognitive artificial intelligence provide interpretable outputs, they are rarely integrated with high-performance models, resulting in a persistent trade-off between accuracy and interpretability \parencite{Ren2025, Nguyen2025, Souvannalath2024}.

Secondly, class imbalance remains a significant challenge in DGA datasets. Although data augmentation techniques such as GAN and ACGAN have been introduced to generate synthetic minority-class samples, these methods often increase model complexity and may introduce uncertainty in the generated data. Furthermore, imbalance-aware models such as SE-ELM improve classification stability; however, a unified and efficient approach that simultaneously ensures data balance, model performance, and computational efficiency is still lacking \parencite{Du2024, Kari2023, Chen2023}.

Thirdly, the generalization capability of existing models remains limited. Many models are developed and validated using specific datasets, which restricts their applicability under varying transformer operating conditions. Previous studies have shown that model performance is highly dependent on dataset characteristics and data distribution, highlighting the need for more robust validation strategies and generalizable models \parencite{Wang2023, Zhou2024, Laburu2024}.

Finally, although recent studies have explored explainable and cognitive AI approaches, there is still limited integration between machine learning models and the underlying physical knowledge of transformer fault mechanisms. Most existing approaches prioritize predictive performance without ensuring that model outputs are consistent with known gas generation behavior, which is essential for reliable engineering applications.

Therefore, a significant research gap exists in the development of an integrated machine learning-based DGA fault classification framework that simultaneously achieves high classification accuracy, ensures model interpretability, effectively handles imbalanced and limited data, and aligns model predictions with physical fault mechanisms. Addressing these challenges is essential for developing reliable, robust, and practically deployable transformer diagnostic systems.
