\section{Overview of Transformer Fault Diagnosis Using DGA}
Power transformers are essential components in electrical power systems, and their reliable operation is critical for maintaining system stability. Internal faults such as thermal degradation, partial discharge, and arcing can progressively weaken insulation systems, eventually leading to catastrophic failures. Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA) has been widely adopted as a condition monitoring technique because it enables early detection of such faults by analyzing gases generated within transformer oil under abnormal conditions. These gases, including hydrogen, methane, ethylene, and acetylene, are closely associated with specific fault mechanisms, making DGA a valuable diagnostic tool (Liu et al., 2025; Nguyen et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023).

However, traditional DGA interpretation techniques, such as the Rogers ratio and Duval Triangle methods, rely on fixed threshold values and predefined decision rules, which limit their effectiveness in complex real-world scenarios. Peng et al. (2024) highlighted that early warning systems based on conventional methods often fail to adapt to evolving operating conditions, while Liu et al. (2025) emphasized that fault patterns may overlap across categories, leading to ambiguous diagnosis. Similarly, Wang et al. (2023) demonstrated that rule-based approaches struggle when dealing with noisy and imbalanced datasets. These limitations have driven the need for more flexible and data-driven diagnostic techniques (Peng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023).

\section{Emergence of Machine Learning in DGA-Based Fault Classification}
Machine learning (ML) has emerged as a powerful alternative to traditional DGA interpretation methods due to its ability to learn complex nonlinear relationships directly from data. In transformer diagnostics, gas generation patterns are influenced by multiple interacting factors, making ML models particularly suitable for capturing hidden relationships that cannot be represented using fixed rules. Nguyen et al. (2025) proposed an adaptive multi-fuzzy logic approach that improves classification flexibility, while Liu et al. (2025) demonstrated that self-organizing neural networks can effectively model DGA patterns for fault diagnosis. Wang et al. (2023) further showed that hybrid AI models can outperform conventional approaches, particularly in imbalanced datasets (Nguyen et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023).

Recent studies have also focused on improving ML model performance under practical constraints such as limited and imbalanced data. Ren et al. (2025) introduced a CatBoost-based framework optimized using SCGWO to improve fault classification accuracy, particularly for minority fault classes. Similarly, Du et al. (2024) proposed a reduced-dimensional generative adversarial network (GAN) to enhance sample diversity and address class imbalance in DGA datasets. Kari et al. (2023) further improved this approach using an enhanced ACGAN model, demonstrating that data augmentation techniques are critical for achieving reliable performance in transformer fault diagnosis. These studies collectively highlight the growing importance of data-driven methods in DGA analysis (Ren et al., 2025; Du et al., 2024; Kari et al., 2023).
\section{Machine Learning Problem Formulation in DGA-Based Fault Classification}
In the reviewed literature, transformer fault diagnosis using Dissolved Gas Analysis (DGA) is consistently formulated as a multi-class classification problem, where input gas features are mapped to discrete fault categories. Ren et al. (2025) proposed a fault classification model based on CatBoost optimized with SCGWO, explicitly addressing multi-class transformer fault diagnosis using DGA data. Similarly, Kumar et al. (2024) evaluated multiple machine learning algorithms for transformer fault classification, confirming that classification-based approaches are widely applied in DGA diagnostics. Nguyen et al. (2025) further applied adaptive fuzzy logic systems to classify transformer faults based on gas concentrations, reinforcing the classification formulation across different modeling approaches (Ren et al., 2025; Kumar et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2025).

In addition to supervised classification, several studies have addressed the limitation of labeled data by introducing semi-supervised learning frameworks. Zhou et al. (2023) developed a semi-supervised graph convolutional network (GCN) for transformer fault diagnosis, enabling the use of both labeled and unlabeled DGA data. Similarly, Sun et al. (2025) proposed a dual-stage semi-supervised learning approach to improve diagnostic performance under limited labeled conditions. Liu et al. (2025) applied self-organizing neural networks, which capture data structure without relying entirely on labeled data. These studies indicate that DGA-based fault diagnosis is also treated as a limited-label learning problem, where the availability of labeled data is a critical constraint (Zhou et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025).

A consistent issue identified across the reviewed studies is that DGA-based classification is inherently an imbalanced learning problem. Du et al. (2024) addressed this by proposing a reduced-dimensional generative adversarial network to generate synthetic minority-class samples, improving class distribution. Kari et al. (2023) applied an improved ACGAN model to balance DGA datasets and enhance diagnostic reliability. Chen et al. (2023) further formulated transformer diagnosis as a highly imbalanced multiclass problem and proposed a sequential ensembled extreme learning machine (SE-ELM) to improve classification performance under such conditions. These findings confirm that handling class imbalance is a core aspect of the machine learning problem in DGA-based transformer fault diagnosis (Du et al., 2024; Kari et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
\section{Machine Learning Workflow for DGA Analysis}
In practice, ML-based DGA fault classification follows a structured workflow that ensures consistent and reliable performance. This workflow typically begins with data acquisition, where DGA measurements are collected from transformer monitoring systems. The raw data are then preprocessed to handle missing values, reduce noise, and normalize features. Feature engineering is particularly important in DGA analysis, as raw gas concentrations are often transformed into gas ratios or multidimensional representations to improve fault discrimination. Peng et al. (2024) demonstrated that multi-dimensional gas representation enhances early warning capability, while Lin et al. (2026) emphasized the importance of feature selection in improving model performance. Ren et al. (2025) further showed that combining feature engineering with optimization techniques leads to more accurate and stable models (Peng et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2026; Ren et al., 2025).

Feature selection and dimensionality reduction are critical steps in handling high-dimensional DGA datasets. Zhou et al. (2023) proposed a semi-supervised graph convolutional network (GCN) to improve learning from limited labeled data, while Sun et al. (2025) introduced a dual-stage semi-supervised learning framework to enhance performance under data scarcity. In addition, Lin et al. (2026) applied manifold-based feature selection to identify the most informative gas features, improving both classification accuracy and generalization capability. These studies demonstrate that effective feature engineering and selection are essential components of the ML workflow in transformer fault diagnosis (Zhou et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2026).

\section{Categories of Machine Learning Tasks in Transformer Diagnostics}
Machine learning applications in transformer fault diagnosis can be broadly categorized into classification, regression, and anomaly detection tasks. Classification is the most widely studied approach, where the objective is to identify fault types based on DGA data. Ren et al. (2025) and Kumar et al. (2024) both focused on classification models to distinguish between different fault categories, while Peng et al. (2024) applied classification techniques for early fault warning. These approaches are particularly useful for maintenance decision-making, as they provide clear identification of fault types (Ren et al., 2025; Kumar et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024).

In addition to classification, regression and anomaly detection techniques have also been explored. Zhou et al. (2023) and Sun et al. (2025) applied semi-supervised learning approaches to handle limited labeled data, enabling models to learn from both labeled and unlabeled samples. Meanwhile, Souvannalath et al. (2024) introduced a cognitive AI framework that incorporates contextual understanding for improved diagnostic performance. Vidal and Castro (2023) further proposed a hybrid model combining variational autoencoders and neural networks to enhance feature representation and classification accuracy. These studies demonstrate the expanding role of advanced ML techniques in transformer diagnostics (Zhou et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2025; Souvannalath et al., 2024)

\section{Feature Representation and Characteristics in DGA Analysis}
In DGA-based transformer fault classification, features represent the physical characteristics of transformer conditions, derived from gas generation processes. Liu et al. (2025) demonstrated that gas concentrations reflect underlying fault mechanisms, while Nguyen et al. (2025) showed that fuzzy logic models can capture the relationship between gas features and fault categories more effectively. Wang et al. (2023) further emphasized that the interaction between multiple gas components is nonlinear, requiring advanced feature representations to accurately model fault conditions (Liu et al., 2025; Nguyen et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2023).

To improve feature representation, several studies have proposed advanced feature engineering and selection techniques. Peng et al. (2024) introduced a multi-dimensional gas representation approach to capture complex relationships between features, improving early fault detection performance. Lin et al. (2026) applied manifold-based feature selection to identify the most informative gas features, reducing redundancy and improving classification accuracy. Ren et al. (2025) further demonstrated that optimized feature sets, when combined with CatBoost, significantly enhance classification performance, particularly in high-dimensional datasets. These studies highlight the importance of selecting meaningful features that reflect underlying fault mechanisms (Peng et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2026; Ren et al., 2025).

In addition to traditional feature engineering, recent work has explored latent feature learning techniques. Vidal and Castro (2023) used variational autoencoders (VAE) to learn compact feature representations from DGA data, improving classification performance. Zhou et al. (2023) applied graph-based learning to capture structural relationships between data points, while Sun et al. (2025) leveraged semi-supervised learning to extract features from both labeled and unlabeled data. These approaches demonstrate a shift toward more advanced representation learning techniques that go beyond simple gas ratios and enable more accurate modeling of transformer fault behavior (Vidal & Castro, 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2025).
\section{Datasets Used in DGA-Based Transformer Fault Classification}
The effectiveness of ML-based transformer fault diagnosis is highly dependent on the datasets used for training and evaluation. Most studies rely on DGA datasets consisting of gas concentration measurements and corresponding fault labels obtained from historical records or expert analysis. Ren et al. (2025) utilized DGA datasets with multiple fault categories to evaluate their CatBoost-based model, while Kumar et al. (2024) compared multiple ML algorithms using similar datasets. Wang et al. (2023) further highlighted that dataset quality and distribution significantly affect model performance (Ren et al., 2025; Kumar et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023).

A major challenge identified in these datasets is class imbalance, which can bias model predictions. Du et al. (2024) addressed this issue by generating synthetic samples using GAN-based techniques, improving the representation of minority classes. Kari et al. (2023) similarly applied ACGAN to balance the dataset and improve diagnostic performance. Chen et al. (2023) further demonstrated that imbalance-aware models such as SE-ELM can improve classification accuracy for both majority and minority fault classes. These findings highlight the importance of dataset preprocessing and augmentation in developing reliable ML models (Du et al., 2024; Kari et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
\section{Challenges in ML-Based DGA Fault Classification}
Despite the promising performance of ML-based approaches, several challenges remain. One of the most critical issues is data imbalance, where certain fault types are underrepresented in the dataset. Du et al. (2024) addressed this issue using GAN-based data augmentation, while Kari et al. (2023) improved class balance using ACGAN. Chen et al. (2023) further proposed a sequential ensembled extreme learning machine (SE-ELM) specifically designed for highly imbalanced multiclass DGA classification, highlighting the importance of imbalance-aware modeling (Du et al., 2024; Kari et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).

Another major challenge is model evaluation and generalizability. Laburú et al. (2024) emphasized that traditional evaluation metrics such as accuracy may not be sufficient for imbalanced datasets, and proposed alternative metrics to better reflect classification performance. Zhou et al. (2024) and Wang et al. (2023) also reported that model performance can vary significantly across different datasets and operating conditions, highlighting the need for robust validation strategies. These findings indicate that improving generalization and evaluation methods is essential for practical deployment of ML-based diagnostic systems (Laburú et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023).

\section{Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) and Physical Interpretability}
Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) is increasingly recognized as a critical component in ML-based transformer fault diagnosis, particularly in ensuring that model predictions are interpretable and aligned with physical principles. In DGA applications, this means that the explanations provided by ML models should correspond to known gas generation mechanisms associated with specific faults. Wang et al. (2023) highlighted that black-box models may limit practical adoption due to lack of interpretability, while Nguyen et al. (2025) emphasized the importance of transparent decision-making in engineering applications. Souvannalath et al. (2024) further demonstrated that incorporating domain knowledge into AI systems improves diagnostic reliability (Wang et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2025; Souvannalath et al., 2024).

Recent studies have proposed advanced frameworks that integrate interpretability with predictive performance. Souvannalath et al. (2024) introduced a cognitive AI approach that combines data-driven learning with contextual reasoning, enabling models to provide more meaningful explanations. Vidal and Castro (2023) developed a hybrid model using variational autoencoders and genetic programming to enhance both feature learning and interpretability. Sun et al. (2025) further showed that semi-supervised learning frameworks can improve both performance and understanding of data patterns. These approaches demonstrate that XAI is evolving beyond simple visualization techniques toward more integrated and intelligent systems (Souvannalath et al., 2024; Vidal & Castro, 2023; Sun et al., 2025).

Importantly, XAI in DGA should not only explain model predictions but also ensure that these explanations are physically meaningful. This means that model outputs must align with known relationships between gas concentrations and fault mechanisms. For example, the presence of acetylene is strongly associated with arcing faults, while methane and ethylene are linked to thermal faults. Ensuring that ML models reflect these physical relationships enhances their reliability and trustworthiness in real-world applications. Therefore, the integration of XAI with domain knowledge represents a key step toward developing practical and deployable transformer diagnostic systems (Wang et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2025; Souvannalath et al., 2024).


\section{Transition Toward Intelligent and Explainable Systems}
Recent research has focused on enhancing ML-based diagnostic systems by incorporating intelligent and explainable techniques. Souvannalath et al. (2024) introduced a cognitive artificial intelligence framework that integrates knowledge-based reasoning with data-driven models to improve diagnostic reliability. Similarly, Vidal and Castro (2023) proposed a hybrid model combining variational autoencoders and genetic programming to enhance feature learning and classification accuracy. These approaches reflect a shift toward more advanced and adaptive diagnostic systems (Souvannalath et al., 2024; Vidal & Castro, 2023; Sun et al., 2025).
At the same time, the need for interpretability has led to increasing interest in explainable artificial intelligence (XAI). Wang et al. (2023) emphasized that black-box models may limit practical adoption due to lack of transparency, while Nguyen et al. (2025) highlighted the importance of interpretable models in supporting engineering decision-making. As a result, integrating ML with explainability techniques is becoming an important research direction, ensuring that diagnostic models are not only accurate but also trustworthy and interpretable in real-world applications (Wang et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2025; Souvannalath et al., 2024).
